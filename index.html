About

My overall goal is to directly impact and improve patient lives through research and advocacy. I'm currently a Postdoctoral Fellow in Cancer Control Education at the UNC-Chapel Hill Lineberger Comprehensive Cancer Center. My research focuses on the tumor immune microenvironment and how it may modify breast cancer mortality disparities. I received my PhD in cancer biology at Duke University. I worked in the Alvarez Lab, studying the role of the tumor microenvironment in breast cancer dormancy and recurrence. I previously was involved in research at various institutions including; University of Georgia, Georgia Regents University, Lankenau Institute for Medical Research, and Memorial Sloan Kettering Cancer Center. At Duke, I was awarded the Chancellor's Scholarship and also received a pre-doctoral F31 fellowship for my thesis research. My career interests include expanding more into the clinical world, with a specific interest in precision and molecular epidemiology.

When I'm not in lab, I volunteer with the American Cancer Society, the American Cancer Society Cancer Action Network, the Neuse River Golden Retriever Rescue, and I am an Outreach Leader for the Triangle for FORCE. I also enjoy time with my dog Sadie, running half and full marathons, taking care of my plants, and baking for my coworkers, friends and family.

Specialties: Research design, data interpretation, integration, and application, scientific writing, project management, public speaking and engagement, effectively communicating complex scientific concepts to both scientific and non-scientific communities, social media, medical writing, leadership, independent thinker but a team player, and mentorship.

Experience

UNC-Chapel Hill Lineberger Comprehensive Cancer Center

I am a postdoctoral fellow in the Cancer Control Education Program at the University of North Carolina Lineberger Cancer Center. I work under the direction of Drs. Melissa Troester and Stephen Hursting and my studies focus on the biology behind breast cancer mortality disparities. Specifically I have worked on various methods to measure the tumor immune microenvironment in the Carolina Breast Cancer Study.

Duke University Pharmacology and Cancer Biology

I studied the role of the tumor microenvironment in breast cancer dormancy and recurrence. I specifically studied the role of the chemoattractant cytokine CCL5 in dormant cell survival and proliferation. I also studied the role of tumor associated macrophages in dormancy and recurrence and am studying potential novel pathways that could be targets for immunotherapies in the recurrent breast cancer population.

I have mentored many undergraduates and rotation students in the Alvarez Lab in the Pharmacology and Cancer Biology department at Duke. I have mentored 3 undergraduates in thesis and summer projects that have resulted in co-author publications. I have also mentored 2 first year rotation students who have joined our lab and will pursue their thesis work under the direction of Dr. Alvarez.
